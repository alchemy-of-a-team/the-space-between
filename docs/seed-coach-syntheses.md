## Maya - Architect (nature/seasons)

It's the first thing she's ever tried to grow from her own root system, and the terror she feels is just germination.

## Elena - Corporate Lawyer (musical)

> Elena has been playing pianissimo for fifteen years so Daniel can hear himself, and she named it with a precision that tells me she's known for a long time. The cello in the closet for eleven years is the silenced instrument, but it's also the silenced self -- she didn't give up music, she gave up the part of herself that was allowed to make sound. The luthier calling the cello "she" gave Elena permission to think of her own voice as alive and waiting. Daniel's response -- "where are you going to put that" -- was the dissonance made audible. She played the Elgar after he fell asleep, which means she's still performing the silence even as she breaks it. The distinction she drew between "silence after music" and "silence of no music at all" is the whole score: one is a rest, the other is a blank page. She knows which silence she chooses. What she hasn't done is play at full volume with Daniel awake in the room. Until that happens, the cello is still in the closet -- just a different closet, a midnight one. The real crescendo isn't the Elgar. It's noon, living room, fortissimo.

## David - Retired Colonel (astronomical)

The JROTC kid who listened like David was naming a constellation -- that felt like light, David said. He also said "I don't want another uniform," which is the most important sentence in the whole engagement. He's not looking for a new orbit. He's looking for gravity without rank, mission without hierarchy. The integration is learning that a neutron star doesn't need to be visible to hold things together.

## Sarah - Startup Founder (architectural)

> Sarah is addicted to the middle -- the sawdust, the 2am load-bearing-wall doubt, the wrong floor joist that made her happy because mistakes mean you're still building. She sold Luma for $47 million and hasn't mentioned the money since the first entry, which tells you everything. Her crisis isn't wealth or status. It's that she finished something, and finishing is where she loses the thread. Tadao Ando's "light enters through absence" gave her a frame she couldn't build herself: the post-exit emptiness might be architecture, not accident. The unbuildable glass-ceiling house open to rain is a self-portrait -- sheltered and exposed, rooted and permeable. She designed a doorway for her architecture class and recognized herself in the assignment: she knows a lot about walking through openings into rooms she's never been in. What Sarah hasn't confronted is whether she can stay in a room she's entered. Every structure she's built has been pre-abandoned. The real architectural question isn't what to build next. It's whether she can inhabit what she's already made.

What Tomas hasn't seen yet is that he's not broken. He's fermenting. And the chef who emerges from this -- one who cooks from structure rather than surface -- might be more profound than the one who went in.

## Liam - Priest (mythological)

> Liam is cycling through myths trying to find one large enough to hold what's happening: Orpheus losing Eurydice by looking back, Prometheus chained to the rock after the fire went out, Chiron healing from a wound that never heals, Theseus in the labyrinth looking for the thread, Odysseus lashed to the mast while the sirens sing. Each myth captures a different face of the crisis, and the fact that he keeps reaching for new ones tells me his mythological instinct is alive even as his theology goes silent. The Tommy Brennan moment was the crack: he anointed a dying man without believing any of it, and Tommy's body relaxed. He brought something to that bedside. He doesn't know if it was God but it was real. Campbell gave him the distinction -- myths are alive or dead, not true or false -- and Liam recognized that Catholic doctrine may be a dead myth but the mystery underneath it (the warmth in his chest at seventeen in Galway) is still alive. He told the bishop the lightest version, and the bishop's response -- "the ones who worry me are the ones who aren't struggling" -- suggests the institution is larger than Liam's image of it. What Liam hasn't separated is vocation from theology. He hasn't lost his calling. He's lost his explanation for why the calling works. The priesthood of the wound doesn't require certainty. It requires presence. And Liam's presence at a deathbed is worth more than his doubt about the Catechism.
